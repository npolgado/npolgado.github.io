A New Chapter

I recently made the transistion from a small autonomy startup to Zoox—a much larger one. At my previous company, I worked on retrofitting industrial vehicles with autonomous capabilities. It was scrappy, fast-paced, and I got to get my hands on just about all aspects of the product. Now I'm at Zoox as a Hardware Software Integration Engineer, working on a vehicle that was designed from the ground up for full autonomy. The scale of the problem is completely different.

Small Startup vs. Big Startup

Both companies operate in the autonomy space, but the day-to-day couldn't be more different. At a small startup, you wear many hats. You're writing firmware one day, debugging a sensor integration the next, and managing vendor relations on Friday. There's a real sense of ownership—you can see your fingerprints on everything. The flip side is uncertainty. Resources are thin, timelines are aggressive, and the company's future is never guaranteed.

At a larger company like Zoox, the depth of specialization is striking. Teams are built around very specific problems, and there's real infrastructure—testing facilities, dedicated tooling, structured processes. You lose some of that scrappy, do-everything energy, but you gain the resources to go deep on hard problems.

What stays the same across both is the people. Autonomy attracts a certain kind of engineer—people who are genuinely passionate about the technology and willing to work through ambiguity. The problems are hard no matter the company size.

Why Zoox

What drew me to Zoox specifically was the ambition of the mission—building a purpose-built robotaxi from scratch. Most companies in this space retrofit existing vehicles, which comes with a lot of compromises. Zoox designed their vehicle specifically for autonomous operation, and that changes the engineering constraints in interesting ways. As someone who sits at the intersection of hardware and software, the integration challenges here are exactly the kind of problems I want to be solving.

Looking Forward

This move aligns with what I wrote in my last status update—wanting to go deeper into the areas of electrical engineering that excite me most. Hardware-software integration at this scale touches embedded systems, communication protocols, sensor interfaces, and reliability engineering. I'm still early, but I'm excited about where this goes.
